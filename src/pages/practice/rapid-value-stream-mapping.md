whatIs: S﻿imilar to traditional [Value Stream
  Mapping](https://openpracticelibrary.com/practice/vsm-and-mbpm/),  Rapid Value
  Stream Mapping is a variant from [Flow
  Engineering](https://openpracticelibrary.com/practice/flow-engineering) that
  simplifies the map artifact and blends the strengths of [Event
  Storming](https://openpracticelibrary.com/practice/event-storming/) and
  Metrics-Based Process Mapping to quickly create a 'good enough' map of flow
  and data in 2 hours or less.
whyDo: >-
  I﻿t's often difficult to get buy-in or time for extensive mapping, so Rapid
  Value Stream Mapping is tailored for common circumstances and intended to fit
  within timeboxes teams already have allocated for less productive efforts.


  T﻿he mapping allows for many benefits provided by Value Stream Mapping: Shared visibility of invisible work, data to support decision-making, focus on a key constraint or bottleneck. 


  Combined with the other maps of Flow Engineering, it allows for a quick and complete execution of improvement discovery from initial need or goal, all the way to actionable, owned and measured next steps, by creating visibility and understanding of the current flow of work. 


  I﻿t's common that Rapid Value Stream Mapping creates an immediate 20% reduction in lead time just by creating visibility and focus on easily-removed waste. Combined with improvement efforts and automation, it's common to see 60-80% reduction of end-to-end lead time for large value streams like projects, product development, and incident response.


  T﻿he same practice can be used on any flow of activities, such as:


  * C﻿ustomer or new hire onboarding

  * P﻿roduct development

  * P﻿artner integrations

  * B﻿usiness acquisitions

  * I﻿ncident response

  * S﻿ales process

  * M﻿arketing campaign execution

  * E﻿vent planning
howTo: >-
  The process consists of **five distinct stages**.


  ## 1. Stream Selection


  You cannot map everything. This step focuses the team on the specific sequence of work most relevant to your Target Outcome - what you aim to achieve.\

  \

  **Facilitation tips:**


  * **Anchor to the Customer:** Ask, "Who is the customer receiving the value defined in our workflow in service of our target outcome?" 

  * **Define Boundaries:** Explicitly agree on the start and end points of the stream.

  * **Limit Scope:** If the stream is too large (e.g., "Idea to Cash"), slice it. Focus on the segment most likely to contain the bottleneck preventing the target outcome.


  ## 2. Add Activities


  This visualizes the work. The goal is to capture the reality of what actually happens, not what *should* happen.\

  \

  **Facilitation tips:**


  * **Work Backward:** Start from the customer/end value and ask, "What happens immediately before this?" This prevents missed branches and "happy path" bias.

  * **Use Verbs:** Ensure every sticky note describes an action (e.g., "Review Code," "Deploy Artifact").

  * **Keep it Rough:** Discourage perfectionism. If a debate arises about a specific step order, note it as a variation and move on.


  ## 3. Add Timing


  This adds the dimension of *flow* to the map, revealing where time is actually spent versus where value is created.\

  \

  **Facilitation tips:**


  * **Two Key Metrics:** Focus primarily on **Process Time** (or Cycle Time, the start-to-finish time for each activity) and **Wait Time** (queue time/delays/waiting).

  * **Use Ranges:** Teams often freeze when asked for exact numbers. Ask for best case / worst case to establish the range of "it depends" but press for what happens "most often". 

  * **Calculate Lead Time:** Sum the times to show the total duration, highlighting the efficiency gap (Process Time / Total Lead Time).


  ## 4. Add Other Relevant Data


  Time isn't the only cost. This step layers on additional context needed to understand the friction in the system.\

  \

  **Facilitation tips:**


  * **Quality Metrics:** Ask about **% Complete & Accurate (%C/A)**—"How often does this step receive work that is usable without clarification or fix?"

  * **Tools & Artifacts:** Note *where* the work happens (e.g., Jira, Slack, Excel) to highlight context switching and data exchange.

  * **Frustration Levels:** Use simple indicators (like red dots or frowns) to mark steps that burn out the team, even if they are fast.


  ## 5. Highlight Constraint


  The ultimate goal of the map is to find the *primary* bottleneck. This focus prevents efforts that make conditions worse by overloading an already constrained activity.\

  \

  **Facilitation tips:**


  * **One Constraint Rule:** Push the team to agree on the *single* biggest factor limiting the flow right now.

  * **Visual Focus:** Mark this spot clearly (e.g., with a "storm cloud" or heavy border).

  * **Consensus Check:** Ask, "If we fixed everything else but ignored this, would we still miss our outcome?" If yes, you found the constraint.
